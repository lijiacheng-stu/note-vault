**Zhang Han:** Skill
**Lin Yue**: Creativity


1**Zhang Han:**  
I think child education in China should prioritize skills before creativity. If you encourage creativity too early without basic knowledge, ideas may stay abstract. Skills give children the tools to turn imagination into something real.

2**Lin Yue:**  
I get your point, but creativity isn’t just about having ideas — it’s also about asking good questions. Skills are important, but kids also need to learn how to think. If education only focuses on skills, students may become good at following instructions but weak at independent thinking. 

3**Zhang Han:**  
That sounds good in theory, but we also have to face reality. China’s education system still relies heavily on exams, which mainly test skills. Although not perfect, exams give students from different backgrounds a fair chance. Creativity is harder to evaluate fairly, and focusing on it too early may benefit students with more resources.

4**Lin Yue:**  
That’s fair, but I don’t think exams in China only test skills. In subjects like math, students with creativity often do better because they can find smarter ways to solve problems or think more flexibly. So creativity and skills are not completely separate — creativity can also help improve scores.

5**Zhang Han:**  
I agree that creativity can help students get better scores, but skill practice still matters. Once basic skills become automatic, children have more mental energy for creativity. So skill doesn’t limit creativity — it helps develop it.

**Lin Yue:**  
That’s true, but it also works the other way around. Creativity can help develop skills too. When children are curious or interested in a problem, they often learn faster and more deeply. Sometimes creativity is what motivates children to practice and improve their skills.

**Zhang Han:**  
So maybe the real answer is balance.  Education should develop both.

**Lin Yue:**  
Exactly. Instead of choosing one over the other, schools should connect them from the start. Children should learn skills through meaningful problems, and assessment should value not only correct answers but also originality and reasoning. Teachers should design learning experiences where skill and creativity grow together.

**Zhang Han:**  
Then maybe the question isn’t “which matters more,” but “how to combine them.” Children need both the ability to do things accurately and the imagination to decide what is worth doing. For a country that wants more innovation, that balance seems especially important.
